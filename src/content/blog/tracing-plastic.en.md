When people hear microplastics, they often picture the ocean. My thesis kept pulling my attention upstream, to the rivers and soils and storm drains where a lot of that plastic starts its journey. A catchment is just the area of land that drains to one point of water. Tracing plastic through it means asking a simple question with surprisingly hard answers: where does it enter, where does it pause, and where does it leave.

Three ideas stayed with me through the work.

First, plastic spends most of its life not moving. It settles in sediment, in the slow bends of a stream, in the soil at the edge of a field. The dramatic part is the rare event, a storm, a flood, that picks it all up at once.

Second, size changes everything. Larger fragments behave like the gravel and leaves around them. The smallest particles behave more like the water itself, and that makes them much harder to catch and to measure.

Third, the hardest part is rarely the chemistry. It is deciding what to count, and being honest about what you cannot.

I am still sitting with these questions, and I expect to be for a while. If you work on any of this, in water, in soils, or in the messy space between, I would love to compare notes.
